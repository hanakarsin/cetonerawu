Challenges Facing Devolution in Kenya

April 2024 · 7 minute read

One of the hallmarks of the 2010 Kenya Constitution is the introduction of the devolved systems that run parallel with the national governments. Since then, Kenyans have had a chance to elect representatives in the county government twice (2013 and 2017). The country has overcome several challenges and milestones in a bid to make the devolution dream a reality. Even before Kenya gained independence, there were calls for a regional system (majimbo) that would ensure equal distribution of powers. However, when the ruling party took over, it favored the Provincial Administration system under the central government. With the failure of the Provincial Administration to address pertinent issues at local level, talks on a mechanism of equal distribution of powers and resources began.

History of Devolution in Kenya

In the 1990s, devolution was one of the objectives of the Safari Park National Conferences. The Constitutional of Kenya Review Commission (CKRC) took up the matter during the process of the Constitution of Kenya Review Act (2000). Concentration of power in state (primarily the president)

  • Most of the decision making was away from people and did not mirror the realities at the grassroots.
  • Some areas felt neglected, marginalized and deprived of the local resources (what was rightfully theirs)
  • Local governments had less financial resources and authority to ensure equal distribution of power.
  • People complained about dealing with dilapidated public resources and infrastructure including roads and hospitals which impeded economic growth
  • Lack of diversity in the systems of government (minority communities had little or no representation).
  • Victimization due to political affiliations; if an area did not support the government of the time, they did not receive any attention as well had limited resources.

The Bomas delegates okayed the proposal on devolution, but at the time it failed to reach the implementation stage. The government of the time went with the Wako draft that favored the provincial administration system. Nevertheless, the people rejected the Wako Draft during the 2005 referendum.

Kenya went back to the drawing board, and the Committee of Experts included devolution in the draft constitution of 2010. The difference between the CRCK and Committee of experts’ proposal was on the levels of governance and the number of devolved units. The creation of the 47 counties came from the 46 Districts, and Nairobi Area stated in the District and Provinces Act of 1992.

Chapter 11 of the 2010 Constitution breaks down the aspects of devolution which are

  • Administration; the county executive committee led by the governor undertakes all administrative duties
  • Political element; the county assembly which constitutes elected and nominated members of the county. Its functioning is not similar to that of local government
  • Fiscal Devolution; the Public Finance Management Act outlines sharing of revenues and other financial resources between the national and devolved governments
  • Services: Fourth Schedule of the Constitution lists functions of the national and county government.
  • Public participation: The people are part of the decision-making process at the county level.

Advantages of Devolution in Kenya

So, what is the importance of devolution in Kenya? From the need for equal distribution of resources and powers to public participation, here are some of the advantages of decentralization in Kenya

Supports Economic and Social Growth

With the devolved systems, it is easier to achieve growth both at national and county development. As county headquarters and centers develop, new opportunities for employment and investments arise paving the way for more significant progress. Currently, county governments are implementing growth strategies that aim at improving the living standards of its people. Early 2018, Makueni county launched its Universal Health System to make affordable care achievable in the area.

Equitable Distribution of Resources

Previously, most of the resources including public institutions were in the major urban centers and cities. But, with devolution, each county has equal chances of growth as the share of resources is equal.

Diversity and Inclusion

Today, all people including the marginalized and minorities can reap the benefits of self-governance and manage their development and affairs.

National Unity

By reducing the power of the central government and distributing it equally among the counties, the devolved systems hopes to achieve national unity.

Democracy and Accountability

People at the grassroots get to choose their leaders and directly participate in decision-making processes making room for accountability and democracy.

Informed Decision Making

Decisions made at county level focus more on the local needs.

Disadvantages of Devolution in Kenya

While devolution has had its pros, Kenyans have also witnessed its shortcomings. Some of the weaknesses of decentralization are

It is expensive

The introduction of more seats (67 senators, 47 governors and 2,526 member of assemblies) means that the taxpayer is at a loss since the wage budget increased significantly.

Non-uniform Development

Some counties such as Nairobi, Nakuru, and Mombasa, already had a head start development wise. These counties have better infrastructure and public utilities as well as opportunities for employment and investment. It will take extra efforts and some years for other counties to catch up.

Duplication of roles

Currently, more people are doing the same job. Therefore, the chances of misusing powers and wasting resources are higher. Beginning 2014 to 2016, Kenya witnessed a lot of back and forth between the National Assembly and the Senate. For some, it is hard to distinguish between the role of the senators and the MPs.

Mismanagement of funds

Some county governments have been in the spotlight for misappropriating funds while most counties cannot account for the money allocated.

The rough transition processes

Over the last couple of years, Kenyans have been spectators in the pull-and-push between the National and county governments regarding the mandates of both and allocation of resources. Sometime back, the Council of Governors pointed an accusing finger at the state for derailing the transition process. As the two lock horns, service delivery at county level becomes affected. When the counties took over health docket, there has been a go-slow in the services offered and strikes lasting months. Some analysts think that the transition process is to blame for the problem.

Human Resources gaps

What is the point of having resources, when the people in charge cannot manage? Well, this is the problem facing most of the counties. In fact, the hiring process of county executives and other officials is questionable. Some county governments seem to be rewarding allies, rather than appointing professionals to get the job done.

Lack of resources and capacity for efficient service delivery

The county governments have a problem in the management of current resources. As it is, salaries and wages account for a significant portion of the county budgets. Therefore, compromising other development efforts.

Fiscal Challenges

While the National and county legislature oversees the budgeting process, the National Treasury still has a lot of power and control over the fiscal policy. The county governments have not yet come up with systems or measures to handle county treasury operations.

Inclusion is still a problem

Ethnicity still plays a major role in securing votes and representation in the counties. Some minority communities in areas such as Migori, Turkana, Garissa, Bungoma and Samburu feel left out. Also, the participation of special groups such as the disabled, youth and women has not increased significantly as a result of devolution.

Revenue Allocation

The 15% of National revenue allocated to counties may not be enough to enable them to discharge all their duties. While some counties such as Nairobi and Kiambu collect a lot of revenues, the resources are still not enough for efficient service delivery. However, the counties need to be more accountable before they receive extra funding from the government.

Kenyans look to county governments to drive the desired change in the country. While some counties such as Makueni are in the forefront, some are still lagging behind.
