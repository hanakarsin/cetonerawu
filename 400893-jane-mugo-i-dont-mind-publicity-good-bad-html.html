Private detective Jane Mugo has told off those trolling her following her sensational documentary on BBC dubbed Kenya’s Spy Queen which has since gone viral.

The detective toped Kenyan Twitter trends since the piece was aired on Monday, January 25, with a section of users congratulating her while others threw jibes at her.

Taking to Facebook on Tuesday, January 26, Jane dismissed her attackers saying she does not mind publicity, whether good or bad.

"Thank you Lord, my followers, Kenya, Africa, the world for making me detective number one. Trending world number one. I don't mind publicity good or bad is winning. Most importantly, I encouraged and mentored someone," she said.

The 27-minute documentary, done by reporter Sharon Macharia, showed behind the scenes life of Jane, not limited to her chef testing food before she eats and her vicious dogs led by one named Hitler.

While the detective was showing Sharon her taekwondo prowess, she 'accidentally' fell down, a scene which has since been the talk of the town.

However, Jane called out her online haters for singling out the scene insisting the moves were intentional and were just for fun.

" While the world is cogratulating me for winning (in a ) male dorminated job, iI saw some cheap desperate bloggers vomiting hatred looking for cheap publicity. Not heard you speak about my training range, cases, team, giving back to the community, you concentrate on a joke I made intentionally," she added.

The private sleuth claimed her attackers were people she had investigated while others were jealous of her achievements.

As previously reported, what perhaps makes her story an item worth creating online fodder out of is that while she is arguably one of the country's most celebrated detectives, she has also found herself on the wrong side of the law as a wanted criminal.

In 2019, for instance, she appeared before the court over allegations of threatening to kill someone. She denied the charges.
