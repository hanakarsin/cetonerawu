Kenyan entrepreneur wins global award for championing clean energy

August 2024 · 4 minute read

- 34-year-old Cedrick Todwell was feted during a global gala held in South Africa

- He was awarded for his efforts in Nairobi, Eldoret, Kericho, Meru and Kiambu

- Todwell is instrumental in championing for the use of renewable energy in Kenya as opposed to kerosene and firewood

A Kenyan entrepreneur who won this years' Changemakers Award, Cedrick Todwell is championing for the use renewable bio energy in Africa.

Todwell, a marketer and great social entrepreneur is also the Global Commercial Director of Sistema.Bio and has teamed up with other partners to promote the use of biogas and organic fertiliser.

During the 2018 Tropics Gala Dinner held on Saturday, October 20, in Johannesburg, South Africa, Todwell, 34, was given the award for his active role in championing the use of renewable bio energy in Africa.

He was the youngest Kenyan among other young innovators, political leaders and economists who were feted in an event that brought together approximately 150 global business leaders.

Todwell together with Sistema.Bio founder Alex Eaton, has within a year supplied biogas digesters to over 2,000 farmers in Eldoret, Nairobi, Meru, Kericho and Kiambu.

The company has so far employed over 60 Kenyans who serve as technicians, distributors or agents in different parts of the country.

The Sistema technology includes anaerobic reactor, inlet of 200L, fertiliser outlet and storage, cook stove, 35 metres gas line, H2S filter, pressure relief valve, two water tramps, and protective geotextile.

For the smallest unit (Sistema 6) a person is required to pay deposit of KSh 7,790 with 12 equal installments of KSh 4,443. Price vary depending on size but a customer gets some discount when buying on cash.

“Our product comes with a 10-year warranty and we offer after sale services. For example after installation we visit a farmer after 30 days, second visit after 90 days and third visit after 120 days to ensure the system is working well for the benefit of the client,” he said.

The farmer is only required to pay 20% of the total amount for the bio digester to be installed and then the remaining amount is paid in installments for 12 months. The farmer pays for the amount in installments without being charged interest.

Sistema.Bio is also seeking partnerships with banks, agro vet dealers, Saccos and co-operatives to help achieve its target of selling over 20,000 units in two years.

The company has been sensitising communities living around key forests on conservation matters by encouraging them to use biogas as the residue of the bio digester is used as organic fertiliser.

The new bio digester is environment friendly and comes at a time when the government is planning on how to encourage households to shift from over relying on kerosene and firewood for cooking, to gas.

The Sistema.Bio team has also been distributing avocado seedlings to farmers in different parts of the country and advising them on dangers of over relying on chemicals and acidic fertilisers.

“Most farmers have realised the need for producing organic foods that comes with health benefits and with the new hybrid reactor digester system, a farmer gets cooking gas and residue used on farms as organic fertiliser,” said Todwell.

Kenya Biogas Programme, a government agency, has been supporting the company through the SNV program which offers farmers a subsidy of KSh 9,000 per unit.

It has as well helped in terms of market access and quality control. Kiva Kenya has also been supporting the bio digester project by ensuring farmers don’t pay interest when acquiring Sistema.Bio products.

Most of the material for the bio digester are imported from Mexico but tubes and connectors are obtained from local manufacturers.

The major challenge is the very slow clearance of the containers at the port before the material is released for distribution and installation on farms.

“The major challenge we are facing is the very slow clearance process for containers at the Port which takes sometimes 8 weeks hence the burden of storage charges at the port warehouse,” said Todwell.

He said the government should ensure fast clearance of containers or else continuous delays will scare investors.
